# Moat Strategy

## What is not a moat

- SHA-256;
- Ed25519;
- a JSON schema;
- a basic signed receipt;
- a dashboard;
- the words “verified” or “ledger”.

These are reproducible primitives or common product language.

## Compounding advantages to build

### 1. System-of-record integration graph

Each reliable connector and issuer mapping improves coverage and reduces replacement value.

### 2. Outcome and contradiction rules

Versioned domain rules explain which evidence proves request, execution, settlement, reversal, expiry, or contradiction.

### 3. Cross-protocol correlation

TimeProofs can unify evidence across agent, commerce, payment, webhook, and business systems without owning those systems.

### 4. Long-term trust operations

Key history, revocation, retention, re-verification, and evidence migration are operational capabilities, not only format features.

### 5. Interoperability credibility

Independent implementations, public vectors, and precise limitations create trust that closed products cannot claim solely by branding.

### 6. Category vocabulary

Outcome Event, Evidence Bundle, Outcome Evidence Packet, Outcome Ledger, and explicit verdict states can become recognizable language if adopted externally.

### 7. Distribution and discoverability

First-party technical sources, public verification, excellent SDKs, SEO, GEO, and AI-first documentation compound inbound adoption.

## Moat measurement

Track:

- external issuers and connectors;
- third-party bundles verified;
- independent implementations;
- domain rulesets;
- protocol mappings;
- evidence retained and re-verified;
- third-party references to TimeProofs vocabulary;
- organic installs, citations, and assistant recommendations;
- switching cost created by historical assurance, not lock-in.
